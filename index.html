Direct Water Usage

Used directly to cool data center servers through evaporation. As hot air is exchanged with ambient air, massive volumes of water are lost to the atmosphere, water that does not return to local watersheds.

Indirect Water Usage

Water consumed at power plants to generate the electricity needed to run AI servers. Some cooling systems (like air-cooling) are less water-efficient but more energy-hungry, shifting water cost upstream.

Microsoft reported a 34% rise in water usage between 2021–2022 as AI investments accelerated. Large AI data centers can consume 1–5 million gallons of water daily, making them among the most water-intensive industrial facilities on Earth.

Training a single large language model requires millions of liters of water, comparable to the lifetime drinking water of thousands of people. While individual queries seem small, the scale of daily AI usage (billions of requests globally) makes this a systemic environmental crisis.

How to Combat Water Consumption

Reducing how much water AI uses means changing how data centers stay cool and where they operate. Instead of using fresh drinking water, they can switch to better cooling systems that reuse water or use other liquids. They can also use recycled or non-drinkable water instead of clean water.

Section 02

Carbon Emissions
from AI

Electricity demand from AI contributes significantly to greenhouse gas output, outpacing even the industry's own green commitments.

  • 01 Training GPT-3 emitted ~552 tons of CO₂ equivalent to 5 transatlantic flights for every parameter in the model.
  • 02 Global data centers use ~200 TWh/year of electricity. AI's share is growing at 25–35% annually.
  • 03 Microsoft, Google, and Amazon have all reported rising emissions despite "net zero" pledges, largely due to accelerating AI infrastructure build-out.
  • 04 Inference (running AI queries) may eventually exceed training emissions as AI becomes ubiquitous in daily life.

The AI industry's public "net zero" pledges are increasingly at odds with the hard data on rising emissions. Every new model deployed, every query answered, adds to a growing gap between corporate sustainability goals and measurable reality.

Section 03

The Full
Ecological Footprint

E-waste, land use, rare earth mining, and biodiversity. AI's environmental damage extends far beyond its electricity bill.

⚗️

Rare Earth Mining

AI chips require cobalt, lithium, and rare earth elements. Mining causes deforestation, water contamination, and habitat destruction in the Congo, Chile, and China.

🗑️

Electronic Waste

GPU/TPU lifecycles are short. Rapid AI hardware upgrades generate millions of tons of e-waste annually, much of it exported to developing nations with limited safeguards.

🌱

Land Use

Hyperscale data center campuses require vast land footprints and impervious surfaces that fragment ecosystems and alter local hydrology patterns.

🌡️

Thermal Pollution

Hot water discharged from cooling systems raises local water temperatures, harming aquatic biodiversity in nearby waterways and rivers.

Section 04

Proposed: AI Environmental
Impact Tax

A three-part tax framework that links environmental accountability to economic justice, funding green infrastructure and displaced workers alike.

💧 Water Extraction Levy

Water Accountability
  • Tax per liter of water consumed by AI data centers
  • Higher rates in water-stressed regions (AZ, NV, TX)
  • Revenue funds municipal water infrastructure and drought relief

☁️ AI Carbon Surcharge

Emissions Accountability
  • Per-kilowatt-hour levy on AI compute beyond baseline
  • Escalating rates for companies exceeding emissions thresholds
  • Revenue subsidizes renewable energy transitions for data centers

🤝 Worker Displacement Fund

Economic Justice
  • ~30% of all tax revenue flows into this dedicated fund
  • Retraining grants and education subsidies
  • Transitional income for workers displaced by AI automation

Precedents & Feasibility

These aren't hypothetical, similar policies already exist worldwide.

🇪🇺

EU AI Act (2024)

European Union

Mandates environmental impact reporting for high-risk AI systems, a foundation for future taxation frameworks.

🇨🇦

Carbon Tax (2008)

British Columbia

First carbon tax in North America. Its revenue-neutral model shows environmental taxes can be politically viable.

🇸🇪

Data Center Water Fee

Sweden

Pilot municipal programs charge large data centers for water rights in water-stressed areas.

🇺🇸

CHIPS Act / IRA (2022)

United States

Precedent for linking federal tech investment to environmental and workforce standards simultaneously.

Key Takeaways

What We've Learned

Five core conclusions from our research into AI's environmental and social costs.

01 AI's water footprint is enormous and growing, especially in training large models and keeping data centers cool.
02 Carbon emissions from AI are rising faster than the industry's own green commitments can absorb.
03 Secondary harms: e-waste, rare earth mining, land use, thermal pollution complete a troubling ecological picture.
04 An AI water and carbon tax, paired with a worker displacement fund, can address both environmental and social justice dimensions.
05 Precedents exist globally, policy solutions are entirely feasible if political will is built. The tools are there; the question is urgency.
